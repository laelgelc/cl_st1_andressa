Below I treat the negative pole of Factor 1 (f1_neg) as its own discourse dimension, without pos/neg “versus” framing.

Because this factor is extremely sparse lexically (only one loading item: *script* with loading −.31) and the example texts all have the same very small score (1), the interpretation has to lean heavily on (a) which *source* is driving this pole and (b) the discourse functions visible in the excerpts.

---

## 1. Which group is driving this pole?

Mean scores on Factor 1 (overall):

- generic_gpt: 13.99  
- summary_guided_gpt: 7.75  
- human: 6.16  

The *negative* pole is, by definition, the low end of this factor. Humans have the lowest mean score, so:

- Human posts are the main driver of the **negative** pole of Factor 1.
- Generic GPT posts are maximally *unlike* this pole (they cluster at the positive end).

So whatever this pole encodes, it is a discourse style that is **characteristic of human self-disclosure posts and relatively underrepresented in GPT outputs**, especially under the generic prompt.

## 2. Lexical loading: *script*

The only loading item is:

- *script* (loading −.31)

This is weak and we don’t see *script* in the top-scoring negative examples. That suggests:

- The factor is not a clear “lexical field” dimension (e.g. not about religion, work, etc.).
- Instead, it likely reflects a **stylistic / discourse-organisational** contrast that happens to correlate with *script* in the full corpus, but is not lexically obvious in the 10 sample texts.

So we must give equal weight to the examples as instructed, and treat *script* as a faint clue rather than the core of the dimension.

## 3. Discourse patterns in the high-scoring negative examples

All 10 examples are human-authored. They are short, but they show consistent discourse functions:

### 3.1 Direct, unfiltered emotional outburst

Example 1:
> And disgusting and overthinking and addicted all the fucking time. I hate it so much...

Example 7:
> I can’t handle it anymore, I really miss my social life and how I used to be 

Example 6:
> And now suddenly the endless lonely monotony of the future is laid out in front of you. It is a terrible feeling

Example 9:
> Im lonely and alone from now until Sunday November 1st. Please help. I'm lonely and alone and alone and lonely and lonesome loneliness is lonely. Help

Features:

- Intensifiers and expletives: *all the fucking time*, *I hate it so much*, *I can’t handle it anymore*.
- Repetition and piling up of synonyms: *lonely and alone and alone and lonely and lonesome loneliness is lonely*.
- Minimal narrative scaffolding: the posts drop the reader straight into a feeling state rather than building a story.

This is **raw, unstructured affective venting** rather than polished narrative.

### 3.2 Direct appeals and invitations to respond

Example 2:
> Feel free to DM

Example 5:
> I’m curious to know:  
>  
> Have you personally experienced any peace, relief, or change through Astaghfar or Tahajjud?  
>  
> Please share sometimes one story can strengthen many hearts.

Example 8:
> If anyones in a similar boat…idk, don’t be a stranger

Example 9:
> Please help. … Help

Features:

- Imperatives and invitations: *Feel free to DM*, *Please share*, *don’t be a stranger*, *Please help*.
- Explicit orientation to the reader as a potential responder, not just a passive audience.
- Often minimal self-description; the communicative goal is to **elicit contact, stories, or support**.

This is a **contact-seeking, interactional** discourse.

### 3.3 Everyday, colloquial, sometimes playful register

Example 3:
> today flopped but ill girlboss my way back into consistency again tomorrow

Example 4:
> Chill discord made to just be themselves and chat and be goofy 

Example 8:
> I wanna be a father so bad! … we’re the idiots who’ve assigned meaning to it. … If anyones in a similar boat…idk, don’t be a stranger

Example 10:
> From my dog and stray dogs. Oh, and from AIs. The only words I can get.

Features:

- Internet slang / meme-ish language: *girlboss*, *chill discord*, *be goofy*, *idk*.
- Contractions and non-standard orthography: *wanna*, *ill* (for *I’ll*), *anyones*.
- Casual, spoken-like rhythm, often with abrupt shifts (*Oh, and from AIs.*).

This is **informal, conversational, and idiosyncratic**, not “scripted” or polished.

### 3.4 Existential and evaluative stance

Example 8:
> I don’t think **”we’re all here for a reason, etc”** or any of that nonsense. I think life is a just a series of meaningless chemical & biological accidents, & we’re the idiots who’ve assigned meaning to it. 

Example 6:
> the endless lonely monotony of the future … It is a terrible feeling

Features:

- Strong stance-taking about meaning, purpose, and the future.
- Evaluative lexis: *nonsense*, *meaningless*, *terrible*.
- But still embedded in a personal, first-person frame (*I don’t think… I think life is…*).

So the pole also encodes **subjective, existential evaluation**, but expressed in a personal, colloquial voice.

---

## 4. How this contrasts with GPT outputs (without framing it as a “versus” label)

Given the mean scores, generic GPT posts sit far from this negative pole. That implies that, relative to these human texts, GPT outputs are:

- Less likely to show **unfiltered emotional repetition** and expletives.
- Less likely to use **idiosyncratic slang** like *girlboss*, *goofy* in this context.
- More likely to be **structured, “scripted” self-disclosures** with clearer narrative arcs and balanced tone.

The presence of *script* as the only loading item fits this: the negative pole is where texts are **least like a “script”**—they are more spontaneous, messy, and interactional.

---

## 5. Proposed labels for f1_neg

Below are several candidate labels, each justified by the patterns above. All refer to this pole only.

### Label 1: “Spontaneous, interaction-seeking self-disclosure”

Justification:

- **Spontaneous**: abrupt emotional outbursts, repetition, minimal narrative scaffolding (*Im lonely and alone… Help*; *today flopped but ill girlboss my way back…*).
- **Interaction-seeking**: explicit invitations and pleas (*Feel free to DM*; *Please share*; *don’t be a stranger*; *Please help*).
- **Self-disclosure**: first-person focus on feelings, desires, and life situation.

This label captures both the emotional rawness and the communicative goal of eliciting contact.

---

### Label 2: “Raw, colloquial loneliness venting”

Justification:

- **Raw**: strong affect, expletives, and negative evaluation (*I hate it so much*; *I can’t handle it anymore*; *meaningless chemical & biological accidents*).
- **Colloquial**: slang, contractions, informal syntax (*girlboss*, *chill discord*, *idk*, *wanna*).
- **Venting**: texts often read as short bursts of feeling rather than carefully structured narratives.

This label foregrounds the emotional and stylistic texture of the pole.

---

### Label 3: “Unscripted, personal outreach discourse”

Justification:

- **Unscripted**: consistent with the negative loading of *script* and the messy, non-formulaic style.
- **Personal**: heavy first-person stance and subjective evaluation.
- **Outreach**: repeated invitations to DM, share stories, or not be a stranger.

This label ties the lexical clue (*script*) to the observed discourse function: these are posts that feel least like a pre-written “script” and most like live, personal outreach.

---

## 6. Which label is most appropriate?

Given the need to integrate both the (weak) lexical loading and the examples, the most encompassing label is:

**“Unscripted, interaction-seeking loneliness self-disclosure”**

- *Unscripted* aligns with the negative loading of *script* and the informal, messy style.
- *Interaction-seeking* reflects the DM invitations, pleas for help, and calls for shared stories.
- *Loneliness self-disclosure* grounds the dimension in the corpus context and the intense, first-person emotional content.

This pole is thus best understood as a **human-driven discourse style** characterised by raw, colloquial, and explicitly outreach-oriented expressions of loneliness, in contrast to more structured, “script-like” self-disclosures that are more typical of GPT-generated texts.
