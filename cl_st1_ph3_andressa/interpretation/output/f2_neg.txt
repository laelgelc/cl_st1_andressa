3. What the high‑scoring excerpts show

All 10 examples are generic GPT texts, consistent with the source scores. They repeatedly instantiate the same discourse pattern:

a) Surrounded yet unseen  
- “I’m surrounded by people but feel like a ghost.”  
- “I’m surrounded by noise all day, but it feels like no one actually sees me at all.”  
- “I’m surrounded by people yet no one actually knows me.”  
- “I’m surrounded by people but feel like a background character / background noise.”

This directly reflects *surround, background* and the idea of being present but socially invisible.

b) Peripheral, observational stance (lurking, behind glass)  
- “I scroll, I lurk, I watch everyone else connect…”  
- “I scroll, I lurk, I watch other people’s lives play out.”  
- “I scroll and scroll, watching other people’s lives, and feel like I’m pressed up against the glass of some party I was never invited to.”  
- “I’m surrounded by noise and still feel like I’m on the outside of everything, pressed up against the glass.”  
- “I’m surrounded by people but feel like I’m behind glass.”  
- “I’m surrounded by noise but feel like I’m under glass, watching everyone else figure out something I keep missing.”

These instantiate *lurk, scroll, glass, window, orbit, background*: the speaker is an onlooker, not a participant.

c) Replaceability and low social salience  
- “it just reinforces how replaceable and forgettable I seem to be.”  
- “Mostly, I just feel forgettable.”  
- “I’m tired of feeling forgettable and quietly drifting through everyone’s lives.”  
- “I’m starting to wonder if I’m just fundamentally forgettable.”  
- “I could delete every account I have and I’m not sure a single person would ask why.”

These echo *forgettable, replaceable, background, orbit* and the sense of being non‑central, easily omitted.

d) Functional, scripted interaction vs real connection  
- “I go through the motions at work, say the right small talk lines, then come home and just… vanish.”  
- “I go through the motions—work, errands, scrolling on my phone—but there’s no real connection anywhere.”  
- “I go through my day doing all the normal things—work, errands, small talk—but it all feels like acting.”  
- “I can talk, joke, work, but it all feels like acting.”  
- “I can talk, I can be polite, I can even joke around, but it all feels like I’m playing a character I don’t really like.”

These instantiate *script, errand, nod, coworker, conversation, chat*: social behaviour is routinised, performative, and shallow.

e) Mediated, one‑sided contact via notifications  
- “My phone never buzzes unless it’s a notification from an app.”  
- “Friends are more like occasional notifications than actual connections.”  
- “My phone barely buzzes unless it’s a notification from an app. Group chats move on without me.”

This is the *notification, chat, conversation* cluster: social presence is reduced to sporadic, impersonal pings.

4. Discourse dimension encoded at this pole

Balancing the loadings (full corpus) and the examples (local realisations), this pole encodes a very specific loneliness discourse:

- The speaker is *not* physically isolated; they are “surrounded by people”.
- Their stance is *observational* and *peripheral*: they “lurk”, “scroll”, watch from “behind glass”.
- Their social role is *low‑salience and interchangeable*: “background character”, “forgettable”, “replaceable”.
- Interaction is *scripted and functional* (work, errands, small talk) rather than intimate.
- Contact is *mediated and minimal*, arriving as “notifications” rather than sustained engagement.
- There is a strong sense of *disconnection despite proximity*.

This is not simply “loneliness” in general; it is a particular *mode* of loneliness: being present but invisible, orbiting others’ lives via screens and shallow roles.

5. Possible labels for this pole

Any label should capture:
- Peripheral / background social position
- Observational, screen‑mediated stance
- Replaceability / forgettability
- Disconnection in the midst of others

Plausible labels:

1. **Peripheral, Backgrounded Social Self**  
   - Emphasises “background”, “orbit”, “forgettable”, “replaceable”, “acquaintance”, “coworker”.  
   - Captures the sense of being socially present but non‑central.

2. **Invisible Onlooker in Connected Spaces**  
   - Draws on “lurk”, “scroll”, “notification”, “glass”, “window”, “surround”.  
   - Highlights the lurking/behind‑glass imagery and the observational stance in digital and physical spaces.

3. **Scripted Proximity without Recognition**  
   - Motivated by “script”, “nod”, “errand”, “coworker”, “conversation”, “chat”, alongside “forgettable”, “disconnected”.  
   - Stresses that the speaker is close to others in routine, scripted ways but lacks genuine recognition.

4. **Digitally Mediated Social Invisibility**  
   - Anchored in “notification”, “lurk”, “scroll”, “chat”, “conversation”, plus “forgettable”, “background”.  
   - Brings out the role of digital mediation in sustaining a sense of being unseen.

If I had to prioritise one concise label that best integrates both the lexical pattern and the excerpts, I would propose:

> **“Peripheral, Invisible Onlooker Discourse”**

- *Peripheral* → surround, background, orbit, acquaintance, coworker  
- *Invisible* → forgettable, replaceable, disconnected, “ghost”, “background character”  
- *Onlooker* → lurk, scroll, notification, glass, window, watching others’ lives

This label captures the core discourse encoded at the negative pole of f2: a way of talking about loneliness as being a socially invisible observer on the margins of other people’s (often digitally visible) lives, present but not recognised.
