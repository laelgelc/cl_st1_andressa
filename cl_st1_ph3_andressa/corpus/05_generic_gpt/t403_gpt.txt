It’s a weird feeling to be surrounded by people and still feel like I’m on mute.

I have coworkers, acquaintances, a couple of old friends I text every now and then, family chats that light up my phone. On paper, I’m not alone. But I go through most days feeling like I’m watching my own life from the outside, like a background character.

I’ve gotten really good at small talk. I can ask about your weekend, laugh at the right times, nod in the right places. But the second the conversation gets close to anything real, I freeze up. I don’t know how to bridge that gap between “How’s work?” and “I feel empty most of the time and I don’t know why.” So I keep it surface-level, then go home and wonder why no one really knows me.

It’s not that I haven’t tried. I’ve joined groups, gone to events, forced myself to say yes when I wanted to say no. I always end up feeling like the extra chair at the table—there, but not really needed. People are friendly enough, but connections never seem to stick. They have their people already. I’m just passing through.

Nights are the worst. The quiet gets heavy. I scroll through social media and see everyone else’s inside jokes, group photos, casual “we” and “us” moments. I know it’s curated and not the whole truth, but it still hits this sore spot I can’t seem to numb.

What scares me most is how used to it I’m getting. The routines of being lonely are starting to feel normal: headphones in, long walks alone, pretending I prefer doing everything by myself. I don’t know if that’s acceptance or just giving up on the idea that it could ever really be different.
