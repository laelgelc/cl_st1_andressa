It’s weird how you can be surrounded by people and still feel like you’re behind glass.

On paper, I’m “fine.” I have coworkers I chat with, a couple of old friends I text every now and then, family group chats that light up with memes and random updates. But none of it feels like it actually touches me. It’s like I’m playing a character that says the right things at the right times, but nobody knows what’s going on underneath. Half the time I’m not even sure I do.

What I miss—what I don’t think I’ve ever really had—is that feeling of being known. The kind of connection where you can message someone something small and stupid because you know they’ll understand the subtext without you having to spell it out. Where you don’t have to rehearse what you’re going to say before you say it. Where silence isn’t awkward, it’s just… shared.

Instead, I overthink everything. I censor myself constantly, terrified that I’ll come across as too needy or too boring or too intense. I’ll type out a message and then delete it because “they’re busy” or “this isn’t important enough.” Then I sit there, staring at my phone, feeling lonelier than before because I’m the one who cut off the possibility of connection.

I see other people forming friendships, going on trips together, having inside jokes, and I feel like I’m watching life happen from the audience. I want to be part of it, but I don’t know how to get from here to there. When I do try to reach out, it feels forced and unnatural, like I’m imitating what a social person would do rather than actually being one.

It’s exhausting, carrying this constant sense that I’m peripheral in everyone’s life, including my own. I keep thinking there must be some point where it clicks, where people stop being acquaintances and become “my people,” but that point never seems to come. It’s just me, orbiting around other people’s lives, trying not to get in the way, and quietly wishing someone would notice I’m there.
