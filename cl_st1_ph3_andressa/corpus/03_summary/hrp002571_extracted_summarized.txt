The writer reflects on the value of solitude, defining it as time without social media or shallow interactions, and says it helps generate beautiful ideas and self-understanding. They argue that while one-on-one conversations can be meaningful, we shouldn’t fixate on lost social connections but instead focus on other “doors” in life, like noticing small details on a solitary walk. They compare social interaction to a dopamine-inducing addiction where loneliness feels like withdrawal, and suggest “necessary loneliness” in moderation is healthy, like limited sugar. True happiness, they say, comes from fulfilling promises to your future self and, if you have others, to them as well—otherwise, to the greater good. Life is described as a long marathon we run alone without an audience, where satisfaction depends on our effort. They end by wishing others well and emphasizing that we’re all in this together, just in different places and times.
