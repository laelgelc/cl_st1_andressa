The post explains that loneliness is no longer mainly an “elderly problem” and is now especially high among young people. A global NIVEA survey shows 1 in 4 people aged 16–24 feel lonely, compared to 19% of all adults, with severe loneliness peaking between 15–29, while adults 65+ report much lower levels (17%). Over half of lonely people struggle to ask for help. Psychologists link this to superficial social media use, economic instability, emotionally shallow digital spaces, and individualistic cultures that weaken community. Middle-aged adults, especially in the U.S., also show high loneliness due to career pressure, caregiving, debt, and weak social safety nets. The poster concludes that loneliness is now a public health crisis and argues for better community spaces, healthier technology norms, and stronger support systems to reconnect people.
