I never realized how much I relied on simply having people around me until it was gone.

In school, in the military, at past jobs, there were always people. I didn’t always like them, I didn’t always feel close to them, but they were there. I took that for granted. Now I’m older, and I’ve been running a startup alone for the last six months. Most days it’s just me and a screen.

What’s strange is that I’m still surrounded by people in public—cafes, trains, streets—but it feels like I can’t really talk to anyone. Not in the way that matters. I want to tell my story, and I want to hear other people’s stories, but when it comes down to it, it feels impossibly hard to cross that line from small talk to something real.

Genuine listening, where both people share and actually hear each other, feels incredibly rare and precious. I keep wondering if loneliness is a kind of poison slowly eating away at me, or some twisted kind of medicine that’s forcing me to see what I really need.

Someone wrote that the cruelest thing you can do is let a person “talk alone.” I think about that a lot. Please, if you can, really listen to someone. It might save a life.
