I live in a pretty conservative, mostly white city, and I stick out whether I want to or not. I’m mixed, heavily tattooed and pierced, and I can feel it when I walk into a room – that subtle shift, the double-takes, the way conversations quiet just a bit. It’s like my existence makes people uncomfortable before I even open my mouth.

Holidays are complicated for me. I grew up with abusive family holidays, so for a long time now I’ve spent them alone. It’s actually become something sacred – my own quiet space where I don’t have to brace myself for cruelty or judgment. Just me, my own little traditions, and no one yelling or picking me apart.

This year I tried to do something different. I’m seeing someone, and their family was having a Christmas party. I thought, maybe it’s time to try being around people again, to not be the person who’s always alone on holidays.

It went… pretty much how my worst fears expected.

No one greeted me when I walked in. People just kind of stared, then looked away like eye contact might obligate them to acknowledge I was there. I tried to smile, tried not to take up too much space, but I just felt like this ugly, unwanted thing in the corner of the room. It was that special kind of loneliness you only feel when you’re surrounded by people.

I left early and took a Lyft home, and the whole ride I just sat there thinking about how alone I really am in this city, and honestly, in life. There are people who fit in, who are easy for others to like and accept. Then there are people like me, who walk alone whether we want to or not.

But the more I think about it, the more I realize: life is often more beautiful when I’m not around other people’s ignorance. My solitude feels safer, more honest. I’d rather be alone than sit in a room full of people who pretend I’m not there.

If you’re also alone – especially during the holidays – I guess this is my way of sitting with you in that. We’re alone, but at least we’re alone together.
