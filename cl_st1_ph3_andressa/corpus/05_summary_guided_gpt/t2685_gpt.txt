I came across a quote from John O’Donohue (who’s referencing Thomas à Kempis) about learning to inhabit one’s aloneness, and it’s been sitting with me.

He talks about how, when we run from our aloneness and chase distraction or a kind of false belonging in society, we actually end up feeling more empty. I recognize myself in that: constantly looking outward, hoping other people or busyness will make the loneliness go away, and then feeling even more disconnected afterward.

What struck me is his idea that if we turn toward our aloneness instead of away from it, something gradually changes. The bleakness doesn’t disappear overnight, but over time it can soften into a sense of inner “home” and real belonging to ourselves. That inner grounding isn’t selfish; it actually makes space for more genuine connection, because you’re not clinging to others for constant affirmation.

He also emphasizes that this is a slow process, sometimes taking years, but that it’s essential for coming into rhythm with your own individuality. I’m trying to believe that sitting with my own aloneness might eventually lead somewhere kinder and more real.
