I’m 32, male, and I feel like I’ve lived a whole lifetime in the past year or so.

Before everything went to hell physically, I was already falling apart mentally. I had what I can only describe as a severe breakdown. I’d go through the day pretending to be fine, then cry at night where no one could see. I felt completely alone long before I ended up in a hospital bed.

Just when I thought I might be slowly pulling myself together, I got hit with a gallstone that led to life-threatening pancreatitis. Suddenly I was in the hospital for almost six months. I couldn’t eat. I had tubes in my stomach and liver. Multiple surgeries and procedures. A serious infection. Time in the ICU. Constant, extreme pain. I lost over 60 pounds and I honestly felt like walking body horror, barely recognizing myself when I caught a glimpse in a mirror.

A lot of the time, I was alone. There were visits, but there’s a different kind of loneliness that comes at 3 a.m. when you’re in pain, hooked up to machines, and you can hear other people moaning down the hall. Everyone kept telling me how strong and brave I was, how inspiring my “fight” was. But that’s not how it felt from the inside.

I didn’t feel strong. I felt numb. I wasn’t fighting because I was courageous; I was just… there. Things were happening to me and I went along because there was no other option. Sometimes, before a procedure, I’d think, “If I just don’t wake up, that might be okay.” Not in a dramatic way, just this weird indifference to whether I lived or died.

Now my body is finally starting to feel a bit better, and somehow that’s making everything in my head louder again. The mental pressure that got pushed into the background by all the physical trauma is coming back. The loneliness, the emptiness, the exhaustion.

Everyone still calls me strong, but I’m so tired of being “strong.” I don’t feel strong. I feel worn out, hollow, and scared of what happens now that the crisis is over and I’m left alone with my thoughts again.
