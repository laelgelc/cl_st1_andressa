Sometimes I still can’t believe how much of my life was shaped by those school years. I was always the one who looked “wrong,” who didn’t fit the mold. People made sure I knew it. I was treated like I was less than everyone else, like I didn’t even belong in the same room.

Classmates would mock me, make fun of how I looked, laugh at me openly. There were days I felt like I was just there to be the punchline. The worst part was that even my best friend joined in. The person I thought I could trust used me when it was convenient, then turned around and laughed with everyone else. That betrayal still stings.

Teachers didn’t help. They’d compare me to the “smart” students right in front of the class, as if I was some kind of example of what not to be. “Why can’t you be more like them?” they’d say, and of course that just gave everyone more fuel to ridicule me, call me names, and treat me like I was stupid or hopeless.

I was struggling academically, but there was no real guidance, no support. Just criticism and comparisons. I felt like I was constantly drowning and everyone was watching, judging, and laughing instead of helping.

All of that slowly dug into me. I developed this deep insecurity, an inferiority complex I still can’t shake. I walk into rooms and automatically assume I’m the least worthy person there. Those memories don’t feel like “just the past.” They’re still with me, like scars I can’t stop tracing.

And yet, back then, I still went to school every day. I didn’t have a choice. I dragged myself there, sat through the humiliation, and then did it again the next day. I don’t know if that counts as strength, but it’s something I carry with me, along with all the hurt.
