I’ve been thinking a lot about how we still talk about loneliness like it’s mostly an “old people” thing, when that just doesn’t match what’s actually happening anymore.

A global NIVEA survey found that 1 in 4 people aged 16–24 feel lonely, compared to 19% of all adults. Severe loneliness peaks between 15–29. Meanwhile, adults 65+ actually report *lower* levels, around 17%. So the stereotype of the lonely elderly person sitting alone is only part of the picture now. A lot of the loneliest people are teenagers and young adults.

What makes it worse is that more than half of lonely people struggle to ask for help at all. So there’s this silent group of young and middle‑aged people who are isolated but don’t know how, or don’t feel allowed, to say anything.

Psychologists point to a mix of things: social media that encourages superficial connections instead of deep ones, economic instability that keeps people in survival mode, digital spaces that feel emotionally shallow, and very individualistic cultures that have kind of hollowed out real community. Middle‑aged adults, especially in the U.S., are also getting hit hard by loneliness – juggling careers, caregiving, debt, and doing it all in a system with pretty weak social safety nets.

It feels less like a private, individual failing and more like a public health crisis. We need better community spaces, healthier norms around how we use technology, and stronger support systems so people aren’t left to deal with this alone.
