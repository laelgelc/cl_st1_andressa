I keep thinking about how plants survive drought. They don’t panic, don’t scream about how unfair it is. They just quietly let go of what they can’t keep alive anymore—leaves, branches, whatever takes too much—and send what little they have to the roots, to what matters most.

On the surface they look worse, like they’re failing. But underneath, they’re growing stronger in ways no one can see. And even when they finally die, they still give something back. They break down, become soil, feed the next thing that grows. Nothing is really wasted—not the waiting, not the suffering, not even the loss.

I wish I could be like that. To stop clinging so hard to everything I’m losing, to trust that even this empty, dry stretch of my life might be doing something invisible in me. To believe that somehow, someday, none of this will have been for nothing.
