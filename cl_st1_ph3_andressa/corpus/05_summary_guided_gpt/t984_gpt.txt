I’ve gotten so good at being quiet that I’m starting to feel like I don’t exist.

On the outside, I’m calm, polite, “low maintenance.” I don’t complain, I don’t bring up how tired I am, how heavy everything feels. I’ve convinced myself that if I stay small and silent, I’ll be easier to keep around. Easier to “love,” I guess. But the more I do it, the more I feel like I’m fading out of my own life. Less like a person and more like a ghost that just drifts through other people’s days.

Nothing really feels like home anymore. Not my room, not my routines, not my own head. I’m always calculating what to say, how to say it, whether I’m being too much. I’m terrified of driving people away, so I tiptoe through every conversation like it’s a minefield. I measure my worth by tiny reactions—how quickly someone replies, how they look at me, if they seem bored. Every small sign feels like a verdict.

The weird thing is, I flinch when someone actually notices me. If someone asks a slightly deeper question, my first instinct is to shut down, change the subject, pretend I’m fine. But inside I’m screaming for someone to see through it, to look at me and say, “You’re not okay, are you?” No one does. No one asks. And I’m too exhausted to push my way into anyone’s concern.

So I just keep quiet. I keep disappearing. And this strange, distant version of “care” I get—the kind where no one really sees me, where my pain goes unnoticed—has somehow become what I accept as love.
