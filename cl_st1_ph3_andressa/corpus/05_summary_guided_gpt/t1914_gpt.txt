I was raised in pretty extreme isolation. My parents genuinely believed that friends were unnecessary at best and dangerous at worst. They tied the idea of friendship to “evildoers” and even to communism, like anyone who wanted close relationships outside the family must be corrupt or trying to drag you into something bad. So I grew up with basically no social contact beyond them, and I internalized this idea that friends were either a liability or a luxury.

As an adult, I never developed what most people would call “real” friendships. Instead, I treated companionship like any other service. If people use friends for company, advice, emotional support, sex, or just having someone to talk to, I figured all of that could be purchased. So that’s what I did. I paid people for their time and their presence. It was simpler, more transactional, and honestly it made sense to me. You pay, you get what you want; you don’t pay, you don’t.

Now I’m older and I’m trying to save money. I don’t want to keep spending on things that don’t seem “necessary,” and that includes paying for people’s time. So I cut those people off. And now I’m feeling what everyone else calls loneliness. I don’t like the word, but I recognize the discomfort, the emptiness, the sense that something is missing.

There’s one person I still pay: an ex who treats what we had like a job. She expects a monthly payment, and if I stop, she threatens to take me to court. So even that “relationship” is clearly just a financial arrangement, not anything genuine.

All of this has convinced me that loneliness isn’t some mysterious emotional condition. To me, it’s just what happens when you’re not willing or able to pay for other people’s time. When the money stops, the companionship disappears. So it feels less like “no one cares about me” and more like “I’m not buying what other people are buying.” And what everyone else calls “loneliness,” I just see as the financial side of being alone.
