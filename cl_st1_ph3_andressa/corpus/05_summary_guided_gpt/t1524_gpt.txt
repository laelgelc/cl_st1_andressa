I’ve been thinking a lot about how much of my loneliness comes from the way I choose to see myself, not just from my actual circumstances. One thing that keeps standing out to me is how destructive a victim mindset and self‑pity can be.

I don’t mean disability, illness, or any real limitation. Those are facts of life, and some of us genuinely have more to carry than others. What I’m talking about is a separate thing: the habit of feeling sorry for yourself, rehearsing how unfair everything is, and making that your whole identity. That mindset doesn’t comfort you, it just traps you. It doesn’t invite connection, it quietly pushes people away.

You can see it in extremes: there are miserable billionaires who have everything materially but live in constant resentment and emptiness. And there are homeless people who, somehow, still find small things to be grateful for, still manage to laugh, still look outward instead of spiraling inward. Their situations are wildly different, but what really separates them is attitude.

That’s what I’m trying to remind myself. Whatever my limitations are, they’re real and deserve compassion—but they don’t have to define my entire inner life. I still have some responsibility for what I do with what I have. I can either feed my self‑pity or try to work with my circumstances as best I can.

Taking responsibility for my own happiness doesn’t mean being harsh on myself. It means being honest: no one is coming to rescue me from my mindset. I can be kind to myself and still hold myself accountable for the way I respond to life. I don’t control everything that happens, but I do have some say in whether I stay stuck in self‑pity or try, step by step, to move toward connection.
