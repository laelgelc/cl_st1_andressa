I’m in my third year of a math–computer science degree. On paper, everything looks “perfect.” I worked myself into the ground, sacrificed weekends, sleep, hobbies, and basically any semblance of a normal youth to get where I am. Now I’m top of my class and apparently even one of the top students in my country. Professors know my name, people praise me, I’m the “smart one.”

And yet I feel completely empty and alone.

People only seem to remember I exist when exams are coming up. Suddenly my messages light up, classmates are friendly, they want to “study together” or “hang out” — but it always somehow turns into me explaining everything, sharing my notes, walking them through problems. Then, as soon as exams are over, they vanish. No invites, no check-ins, nothing. It’s like I’m a walking calculator that gets put back in the drawer after use.

There was even someone I had a crush on. For a while I thought maybe this was something real. They were always talking to me, laughing with me, sitting next to me. I let myself believe that maybe, finally, someone liked me for me. But when exams ended and they didn’t need my notes or help anymore, they stopped talking to me too. That hurt more than any failed test could.

All of this has made me realize I don’t have real friends. I don’t have love. I have people who orbit me when it’s convenient for them, when I can boost their grades. When they don’t need that, I’m invisible.

I keep thinking about what I gave up to get here: weekends, relationships, peace of mind, just being young and carefree. Sometimes I feel like I traded my entire youth for a line on a CV and some compliments that don’t mean anything when I go home to an empty room.

If I could, I’d trade every award, every grade, every bit of “success” for one genuine friend, one person who actually wants to be with me when there’s nothing to gain. Real laughter. Real warmth. Real love.

What’s the point of being “successful” if there’s no one to share it with?
