# Discursive Representations of Loneliness in Human-Authored and AI-Generated Texts: A Lexical Multidimensional Analysis

## Abstract

This study investigates discursive representations of loneliness in human-authored and AI-generated texts using **Lexical Multidimensional Analysis** (LMDA; Berber Sardinha & Fitzsimmons-Doolan, 2025). LMDA is grounded in traditional multidimensional analysis (TMDA), developed by Biber (1988), and uses lexical features only. Classical psychological accounts treat loneliness mainly as an individual affective condition (Weiss, 1973; Perlman & Peplau, 1982). Work in discourse and media studies, however, treats loneliness as socially mediated and relational, because public accounts of loneliness are produced under conditions of visibility and are constrained by conventions of public self-disclosure (Ågren, 2017; Cover, 2016; Imrie, 2018; Oakley, 2020; Barreto et al., 2024). Loneliness has become taboo and “difficult to confess” (Laing, 2016, as cited in Sagan & Miller, 2018, p. 5). This matters for method. If loneliness is partly constructed in discourse, then the object is the patterned language through which loneliness is expressed in a defined domain.

In corpus terms, the study starts from a domain definition. People can talk about loneliness in private (to themselves or to another person). The present study focuses on **public written** talk about loneliness in social media. The domain is **self-disclosure posts** in loneliness-related subreddits on Reddit. This choice matters because it turns a private experience into a public text. Humans swim in a sea of talking and listening (Hayes, Blackledge, & Barnes-Holmes, 2001, p. 3). Speaking is one way experience becomes socially available. This is why self-disclosure in a public writing domain is a relevant site for studying loneliness talk. This is why the domain must be fixed before humanlikeness can be tested.

A comparable limitation appears in research on AI and emotion expression. Large language models can adopt personas and display human-like characteristics such as feelings and emotions (Jiang et al., 2023). Most studies, however, treat loneliness as input context for empathetic responding, then evaluate model replies through human judgments (Welivita & Pu, 2023). That approach can show whether an AI reply is perceived as acceptable, but it does not show what loneliness talk AI produces when it must write as the lonely subject, and it does not show whether those patterns match human self-disclosure. Human ratings cannot show which lexical patterns co-occur across texts, and they cannot recover latent discourse dimensions that organise loneliness talk in a corpus.

These questions require a method that treats each text as an observation and models lexical covariation across texts, without relying on human ratings. These requirements are met by LMDA (Berber Sardinha & Fitzsimmons-Doolan, 2025). LMDA uses lexical features only and applies factor analysis to identify sets of lexical items that covary across texts; the resulting factors are interpreted as dimensions of discourse around a focal issue as represented in a corpus (Berber Sardinha & Fitzsimmons-Doolan, 2025). Each text receives a score on each dimension. These scores operationalize **markedness**, understood here as the degree to which a text is marked on a dimension, as shown by its dimension score. Markedness is not the same as high word frequency. Humanlikeness can then be assessed as similarity or difference in score profiles across dimensions, both between human and AI texts and between the two prompting conditions.

To do this, we compile three aligned subcorpora. The first is a human corpus of self-disclosure posts from loneliness-related subreddits. The second is an AI-generated corpus in which the model receives a summary of each human post and generates a Reddit post based on that summary. The third is an AI-generated corpus in which the model is only instructed to generate a Reddit post about loneliness. This contrast tests whether humanlikeness depends on providing the model with human post content.

To conduct the LMDA, we process all three subcorpora in the same way. We POS-tag each corpus and extract content words. We compute lexical counts and use them for keyword extraction by comparing each subcorpus against the other subcorpora. The resulting keyword tables are entered into factor analysis. The extracted factors are interpreted as discourse dimensions around loneliness via lexical loadings and inspection of high-scoring texts (Berber Sardinha & Fitzsimmons-Doolan, 2025). This yields dimension scores for each text. These scores operationalize markedness, understood here as the degree to which a text is marked on a dimension, as shown by its dimension score. Markedness is not the same as high word frequency. Humanlikeness can then be assessed as similarity or difference in score profiles across dimensions, both between human and AI texts and between the two prompting conditions. 

The contribution of the project is not a claim about whether AI “feels.” It is an empirical description of the discourse dimensions that organise loneliness talk in human self-disclosure, and a comparison of how AI matches or differs from those dimensions under two prompting conditions. The analysis will show which discourse dimensions are shared by humans and AI, where AI diverges, and whether the summary condition increases similarity in score profiles across dimensions. Humanlikeness, operationalised through dimension scores and markedness, is the central comparative outcome.
